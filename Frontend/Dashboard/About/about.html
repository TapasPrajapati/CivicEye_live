About the Project

Understanding crime patterns through data visualization and analysis

Why This Project?

For a country of 1.2 billion people, India lacks a comprehensive public data portal that provides easy access to crime statistics. The National Crime Records Bureau releases an annual report spanning over 600 pages, detailing various crimes recorded across the nation.

These dense PDF documents require hours of manual analysis by journalists, researchers, and policymakers to extract meaningful insights. Comparing crime data across cities and years typically involves downloading multiple PDFs and manually compiling spreadsheets.

Our Solution

This application liberates data trapped in PDFs, enabling you to compare crimes across different cities and years with ease. Our platform allows you to:

  • City-Specific Analysis

    Identify which crimes most affect specific cities

  • Crime-Specific Insights

    Discover which cities have the highest rates of particular crimes

  • Trend Visualization

    Analyze patterns across five years of data (2010-2014) without manual data processing

Data Methodology

Our data comes from India's National Crime Records Bureau, which compiles reports from police stations nationwide. We've processed the five most recent available datasets (2010-2014).

Key Data Considerations

  • City Coverage & Averaging

    For 18 cities not reported in 2010, we calculated averages over 2011-2014 rather than including 2010 as zero, which would distort the analysis.

  • Standardized Categories

    We maintained the Bureau's categorization of crimes (violent, against women, property, economic) while focusing on the most serious offenses for clearer analysis.

  • Women's Crime Rates

    From 2010-2012, we recalculated crimes against women using 2011 Census data for more accurate rates.

  • Sexual Harassment Category

    We created a unified 'Sexual harassment and molestation' category by combining Section 354 and 509 crimes, standardized across years for consistent analysis of these pervasive but underreported offenses.

  • Hurt Category

    We maintained consistency by including 2014's 'Grievous Hurt' subcategories under the broader 'Hurt' classification used in previous years.
